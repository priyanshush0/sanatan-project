atharvaveda




The Artharvaveda Samhita is the text 'belonging to the Atharvan and Angirasa poets. It has about 760 hymns, and about 160 of the hymns are in common with the Rigveda.[173] Most of the verses are metrical, but some sections are in prose.[173] Two different versions of the text – the Paippalāda and the Śaunakīya – have survived into the modern times.[173][174] The Atharvaveda was not considered as a Veda in the Vedic era, and was accepted as a Veda in late 1st millennium BCE.[175][176] It was compiled last,[177] probably around 900 BCE, although some of its material may go back to the time of the Rigveda,[2] or earlier.[173]





The Atharvaveda is sometimes called the "Veda of magical formulas",[178] an epithet declared to be incorrect by other scholars.[179] The Samhita layer of the text likely represents a developing 2nd millennium BCE tradition of magico-religious rites to address superstitious anxiety, spells to remove maladies believed to be caused by demons, and herbs- and nature-derived potions as medicine.[180][181] The text, states Kenneth Zysk, is one of oldest surviving record of the evolutionary practices in religious medicine and reveals the "earliest forms of folk healing of Indo-European antiquity".[182] Many books of the Atharvaveda Samhita are dedicated to rituals without magic, such as to philosophical speculations and to theosophy.[179]
